## 📖 1. Introduction

**Stationarity** is one of the most important concepts in time series analysis.

A time series is said to be **stationary** if its statistical properties remain constant over time.

### ✔ Key Properties of Stationary Data:

* Mean is constant
* Variance is constant
* Covariance depends only on lag

---

## ❓ 2. Why Do We Need Stationarity?

### 👉 Core Idea

Most time series models assume that the **underlying pattern is stable over time**.

If the data keeps changing its behavior, the model cannot learn meaningful patterns.

---

## 🚨 3. Problems with Non-Stationary Data

Non-stationary data causes several issues:

* Presence of trend (increasing/decreasing)
* Changing variance (heteroscedasticity)
* Unstable seasonal patterns

### ❌ Consequences:

* Past data does not represent future behavior
* Model assumptions break
* Predictions become unreliable

---

## ✅ 4. Benefits of Stationarity

When data is stationary:

* Mean remains constant
* Variance remains stable
* Relationships between observations are consistent

### 🎯 Outcome:

* Models like AR, MA, ARIMA work effectively
* Better forecasting accuracy
* Reliable pattern learning

---

## 🔵 5. Types of Stationarity

There are two main types of stationarity:

1. **Strict (Strong) Stationarity**
2. **Weak (Covariance) Stationarity**

---

## 🟣 5.1 Strict Stationarity

### 📌 Definition

A time series is **strictly stationary** if the **entire probability distribution remains unchanged over time**.

---

### 🧠 Explanation

For any time shift:

* The distribution of values remains exactly the same
* Not only mean and variance, but all higher moments remain constant

---

### 📊 Example

If we take sequences:

* (X₁, X₂, X₃)
* (X₂, X₃, X₄)

👉 Their **joint probability distributions are identical**

---

### ⚠️ Important Notes

* Very strong condition
* Rare in real-world datasets
* Difficult to verify

---

## 🟢 5.2 Weak Stationarity

### 📌 Definition

A time series is **weakly stationary** if:

1. Mean is constant
2. Variance is constant
3. Covariance depends only on lag (not on time)

---

### 🧠 Explanation

* Average value does not change over time
* Spread of data remains stable
* Relationship depends only on time gap (lag)

---

### 📊 Example

* Cov(X₁, X₂) = Cov(X₅, X₆) → Same lag (1)
* Cov(X₁, X₃) = Cov(X₄, X₆) → Same lag (2)

---

### 🎯 Why Weak Stationarity is Used

* Easier to test
* Works well for real-world data
* Required for most models (ARIMA, etc.)

---

## ⚖️ 6. Strict vs Weak Stationarity

| Feature           | Strict Stationarity          | Weak Stationarity                   |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| Definition        | Entire distribution constant | Mean, variance, covariance constant |
| Strength          | Very strong                  | Moderate                            |
| Practical Use     | Rare                         | Common                              |
| Model Requirement | Not required                 | Required for most models            |

---

## 🔥 7. Key Takeaways

* Stationarity makes time series predictable
* Non-stationary data leads to unreliable models
* Weak stationarity is most commonly used
* Strict stationarity is mainly theoretical

---

## 🚀 8. Summary

> Stationarity ensures that statistical properties remain constant over time, making time series data stable and predictable.

👉 In simple terms:

* Non-stationary data = Moving target
* Stationary data = Fixed target

---

## 🧩 Bonus Insight

Most real-world time series are **not stationary by default**.

### 🔧 Common Techniques to Make Data Stationary:

* Differencing
* Log transformation
* Detrending
* Seasonal adjustment

---

## 🏁 Final Note

Understanding stationarity is essential before applying any forecasting model.

It forms the foundation of:

* AR models
* MA models
* ARIMA models
